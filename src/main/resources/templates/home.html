The first case helps in the ordinary management of private infrastructure, the objective is to minimize operational costs associated to physical nodes. Admission control is enabled and user concurrency level must be taken into account, thus possibly leading into job rejections if the cluster capacity is not enough to accommodate all the required workload.

In the second case, the interest lies in provisioning a cluster, we do not consider the admission control mechanism here because we are interested in an average workload scenario.

The first case apply when scaling a public cloud-based cluster to respond to a peak in workload. In such a case, the interest is to determine the number of additional on-demand needed to absorb the temporary increase in load.

The second case is useful in order to evaluate the number of VMs to reserve to support the expected average workload, solve for the minimum quantity of machines. In all the public cloud cases we assume that penalties due to job rejections are more expensive than deploying the required machines, hence the admission control mechanism is disabled.
